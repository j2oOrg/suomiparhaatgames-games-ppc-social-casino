Two years in the making

A longer look at how Pop Sync and the studio came together.

Two years ago we registered the company after meeting for late-night project sessions in a coworking space near the harbor. We were trading playlists and game links, and we kept talking about how noisy most play sites felt. We wanted something calmer, a space that felt like a short set rather than a marathon.

We chose the name Suomiparhaatgames because it sounded warm in Finnish and reminded us to stay local and direct. At first it was only a single page that loaded a game for friends. The feedback was blunt and helpful; people wanted clarity, not hype, and they wanted to see the rules upfront.

Year one was spent shaping the mood. We prototyped Pop Sync with a focus on rhythm cues, bright notes, and a steady pace that does not push you to keep spinning. The visual language came from old concert posters and late-night neon in downtown Helsinki. We tuned the colors, background glow, and short looped animations until it felt like a song you could return to.

Year two was about tightening the experience. We reduced the site to one theme so we could label it well, test it with real people, and keep the copy honest. We also built the navigation, disclaimers, and forms to be clean and respectful. There is no paywall and no prize talk because the point is the atmosphere, not the stakes.

Pop Sync itself is our small, focused game. It uses a simple rhythm reel and a pop-inspired palette so the game reads quickly and feels warm, not overwhelming. The tempo is steady, the visuals are bright, and the whole thing is meant to be played for a few minutes at a time, the way you might replay a favorite chorus.

We are still young, but the last two years taught us that clarity is a feature. If you leave with a sense of what the game is, how it works, and why it exists, we have done our job. That is why we continue to polish this single theme before expanding anything else.

How we build the site week to week

Short tasks, fast feedback, and lots of editing.

We still plan around class schedules, so progress happens in short blocks. We keep a shared checklist and aim for one meaningful improvement at a time, even if it is just cleaner copy or a more readable section.

Most of the layout starts on paper. We sketch what the page should feel like, then translate it into HTML and keep the CSS simple so it does not get in the way of the game.

We test on older laptops and phones because that is what we use every day. If something feels heavy, we cut it back until it loads fast and looks calm.

The writing is also part of the build. If a sentence sounds like marketing, we rewrite it. We want the page to read like a person talking, not a brochure.

What we say yes to

Small choices that make the experience feel honest.

We say yes to clear labels, short sessions, and reminders that this is free-play only. Those lines are not just there for legal reasons; they are part of the tone.

We also say yes to a single theme while we are still small. It lets us know the game deeply and speak about it in a way that feels accurate.

We say no to tricks that keep people clicking. There are no forced sign-ups, no surprise paywalls, and no streaks or popups, and we try to keep the play area as clean as possible.

What Pop Sync is trying to be

A quick, readable game that stays friendly.

Pop Sync is our small experiment in making a game feel calm. The reels are easy to read, the notes are bright, and the pace stays steady so you can step away without feeling like you missed something.

We test it on older laptops and phones because that is what we use every day. If the text looks fuzzy or the screen feels busy, we cut it back.

Our short checklist is simple: clear labels, a calm tempo, and honest copy. It sounds basic, but those three things keep us grounded when we want to add extras.
